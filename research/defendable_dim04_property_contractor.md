## Executive Summary

Property, contractor, and workmanship disputes represent one of the most pervasive and financially damaging categories of consumer harm in the United States. Research for calendar year 2024 and early 2025 reveals a multi-billion-dollar pain landscape affecting hundreds of thousands of consumers annually. Unlike financial services, this domain lacks a single centralized federal complaint database, but state licensing boards, the CFPB, FTC, state attorneys general, municipal consumer protection offices, and BBB data collectively paint a clear picture of systemic dysfunction.

**Key headline findings:**
- Home improvement/contractor complaints rank **#3 nationwide** among consumer complaint categories across 41 state and local consumer agencies [^1^]
- **Illinois AG** alone received **2,256 construction/home improvement complaints** in 2024, making it the #1 category for the fourth consecutive year [^2^]
- The CFPB received **~3.19 million total complaints** in 2024, with mortgage and home-related financial products representing significant subsets [^3^]
- California's CSLB referred **10,399 complaints** for field investigation in FY 2024-25, revoked **591 licenses**, suspended **455**, and issued **1,655 citations** [^4^]
- The FTC received **81,925 reports of home improvement scams** in 2024 [^5^]
- Home warranty claim satisfaction is abysmal: only **49% of claimants** reported satisfaction with their most recent claim outcome in a 2024 national survey [^6^]
- Mechanic's lien activity remains elevated, with Texas, Florida, California, Georgia, and Nevada leading in filings [^7^]
- New home builder warranty claims paid totaled **$1.071 billion** in 2024 [^8^]
- Federal contractor-related enforcement actions returned **$2.9 million** to victims of a single home improvement financing scheme (Ygrene) [^9^]

## Part 2: Taxonomy Mapping — Offense Signals

### Signal 1: Improper/Substandard Workmanship

**Description:** Work performed below contractual or code standards, including defective materials, poor installation, incomplete work, or code violations.

**Evidence:**
- Florida fence contractor: Work not initiated for over a year after $2,896 payment; when started, workmanship was poor with rough wooden slats, faulty gate, missing lock mechanism [^1^]
- Arizona construction litigation trends: "Lawsuits claiming defects in workmanship are increasing, as are cases where contractors are accused of hiring underqualified subcontractors" due to labor shortages affecting 88% of construction firms [^18^]
- Texas construction defect claims: Average commercial construction defect claim is approximately **$10.1 million** [^19^]
- Construction defect litigation described as "evergreen" and expected to increase in 2025 due to labor shortages (500,000 worker shortfall since 2023) and hurricane/wildfire rebuilding demands [^14^]
- Tennessee swim spa: $28,000 purchase delivered as used unit with significant yard damage during installation [^1^]
- Associated General Contractors of America: 53% of construction firms experienced project delays or abandonments due to rising costs in 2024; more than one-third faced postponements from supply chain issues [^20^]

**Frequency:** Very High — construction defect claims are the largest area of construction litigation outside worker safety [^19^]

---

### Signal 2: Contractor Nonperformance / Abandonment

**Description:** Contractor takes payment (often deposit) and fails to perform any work or abandons project mid-completion.

**Evidence:**
- Florida cabinet company: Consumer paid $10,237.50 deposit (one-third of total); never received final contract; company refused full refund claiming cabinets were in warehouse [^1^]
- Illinois AG: Consumers reported "complete failure by companies to conduct any work after receiving a down payment" as a top pattern in 2024 [^2^]
- Utah solar company: Admitted to leaving at least **140 Utah consumers** without functional solar energy systems; used deceptive sales practices including fake Google reviews [^1^]
- California CSLB SWIFT team: 217 jobsite sweeps in first 7 months of 2024, opening 969 complaints and finding widespread nonperformance [^21^]
- Minnesota: Licensed residential building and electrical contractor had both licenses revoked for breaching contracts with multiple homeowners [^12^]
- Bucks County monument company: 89 complaints from consumers who paid for headstones but were not delivered for months or years; business owner operated a second location with same pattern [^1^]

**Frequency:** Very High — consistently ranks as top consumer complaint category across multiple states

---

### Signal 3: Unlicensed Contracting

**Description:** Individuals or entities performing contracting work without required state or local licenses, often with no insurance or bond protection for consumers.

**Evidence:**
- Minnesota: Unlicensed Lakeville contractor fined **$48,000** for residential remodeling and roofing without license; unlicensed plumber from ND fined $3,500; St. Peter contractor fined $10,000 for performing plumbing, electric, remodeling without licenses [^12^]
- California CSLB: SWIFT conducted 26 days of undercover sting operations Jan-July 2024, issued 60 notices to appear (NTAs) to non-licensees, 11 citations, 15 stop orders, 80 criminal referrals [^21^]
- Florida Pinellas County: Multi-day unlicensed contractor stings after 2024 hurricanes led to **arrests of over 100 suspects** with **over 150 criminal charges**; charge enhanced from misdemeanor to felony during state of emergency [^1^]
- Nevada: Non-licensee citations carry civil penalties up to **$15,000** [^15^]
- California CSLB: FY 2024-25 issued 727 non-licensee citations and referred 1,066 cases to prosecutors [^4^]

**Frequency:** High — endemic in post-disaster markets and home improvement sectors

---

### Signal 4: Deceptive Financing and Predatory Lending

**Description:** High-pressure financing tactics, forged liens, deceptive pricing, and predatory loan terms attached to home improvement work.

**Evidence:**
- **FTC v. Ygrene Energy Fund:** Home improvement financing company made false claims that financing would not interfere with home sale/refinancing; contractors used high-pressure sales tactics and **forgery** to sign up consumers; resulted in liens recorded without informed consent; **$3 million settlement**, **$2.9 million in refunds to 960 consumers** [^9^]
- Virginia solar panel case: Contractor employed aggressive tactics to pressure consumer into $45,000 financing for 7.2-kilowatt system; market price should have been ~$21,600; after regulator intervention, contract reduced to $28,000 — **saving consumer $17,000** [^1^]
- Arkansas predatory loan: Consumer took $3,000 loan; after paying $7,374 over two years, principal balance decreased by only **$10**; lender waived remaining $7,308 after AG intervention [^1^]

**Frequency:** Moderate-High — increasingly prevalent with solar and PACE-style financing growth

---

### Signal 5: Home Warranty Claim Denials and Coverage Failures

**Description:** Home warranty companies denying legitimate claims, citing pre-existing conditions, improper maintenance, or fine-print exclusions to avoid payout.

**Evidence:**
- 2024 national survey: Only **49% of home warranty claimants** were satisfied with their most recent claim outcome; many encountered denials or only partial coverage [^6^]
- FTC 2023 action against **Choice Home Warranty**: Alleged deceptive claim denials including citing pre-existing conditions or improper maintenance [^6^]
- BBB tracking: Recurring complaints about denied claims, fine-print exclusions, delays, and cancellation/refund disputes [^6^]
- BBB alert for Like New Home Warranty: "Pattern of consumer complaints alleging misleading sales tactics, failure to cancel contracts, failure to provide refunds" [^22^]
- American Home Shield: "Thousands of complaints" filed with BBB; NBC News investigation documented claim denials [^23^]
- Maryland case: Homeowner hired plumber at warranty company's suggestion; plumber was unlicensed and damaged heating unit, requiring full furnace replacement [^1^]

**Frequency:** Very High — home warranty industry has systematically poor claim satisfaction

---

### Signal 6: Mechanics Lien Abuse and Payment Disputes

**Description:** Contractors filing improper liens, disputes over payment timing/amounts, and retaliatory lien filings.

**Evidence:**
- NCS Credit Lien Index Q3 2025: Index dropped to 51 (from 61 in Q2), but activity remains elevated; top states by volume: **Texas, Florida, California, Georgia, Nevada** [^7^]
- Construction industry: "Rising costs, labor shortages, and market uncertainty make strong credit management essential. Serve notices, secure mechanic's liens and bond claims" [^7^]
- Nearly one in four contractors report tariffs directly led to project delays or cancellations, increasing payment disputes [^7^]
- Arkansas tree service: Company threatened 20% fee for nonpayment, legal action, and **placing a lien on consumer's home** after failing to provide written contracts or prior quotes [^1^]

**Frequency:** Moderate — cyclical with economic conditions; elevated during economic stress

---

### Signal 7: Failed Inspections and Permit Violations

**Description:** Work performed without proper permits, failure to pass inspection, or code violations discovered during or after construction.

**Evidence:**
- California CSLB: 217 SWIFT sweeps across 43 counties and 174 cities Jan-July 2024; issued 106 stop orders and 447 advisory notices for permit/inspection violations [^21^]
- California: New subcontracting restrictions effective Jan 1, 2024 (Business and Professions Code Section 7035) to prevent misclassification and ensure compliance [^24^]
- CSLB formed **task force dedicated to investigating "rented qualifiers"** — individuals who lend their license name without actual supervision [^24^]
- Pinellas County post-hurricane: Contractors investigated for unlicensed work; penalties enhanced to felony during state of emergency [^1^]

**Frequency:** Moderate-High — particularly acute in post-disaster rebuilding and unlicensed work

---

### Signal 8: Post-Disaster Fraud and Emergency Scams

**Description:** Contractors exploiting homeowners after natural disasters with emergency repairs, inflated pricing, and unlicensed work.

**Evidence:**
- Pinellas County Florida (2024 hurricane season): Participated in planning and implementation of two unlicensed contractor stings; arrests of **100+ suspects**, **150+ criminal charges** [^1^]
- Arkansas Capital City Tree Service: After severe weather events in 2023-2024, took advantage of consumers by overcharging, failing to provide quotes, failing to work with insurance companies, threatening liens and legal action [^1^]
- Scam pattern: Contractors offering "free electrical inspections" after storms, claiming entire panel needs replacement for $8,000-$12,000 when only $200 GFCI outlet replacement was needed [^5^]

**Frequency:** Spikes significantly after major weather events; endemic in disaster zones

## Part 4: Taxonomy Mapping — Defense Fixers

### Fixer 1: Pre-Hiring Verification Protocol

**Description:** Systematic verification of contractor credentials before engagement.

**Actions:**
- Verify license status through state contractor licensing board (CSLB, CILB, NSCB, etc.)
- Check disciplinary history and complaint record
- Confirm insurance (general liability, workers' compensation) and bonding
- Request and contact references from recent jobs
- Obtain multiple written estimates (CFA recommends minimum 3)
- Never pay full amount upfront; reasonable deposits are 10-20% [^26^]

---

### Fixer 2: Contract Documentation and Compliance

**Description:** Ensuring written contracts meet all statutory requirements.

**Actions:**
- California requires specific contract elements: payment schedule, start/completion dates, 3-day right to cancel, accurate deposit limits, Mechanics Lien Warning [^17^]
- Do not begin work without signed written contract
- Ensure contract specifies scope, materials, timeline, payment milestones
- Florida: Check contractor registration status with state
- Tennessee: Verify license through TN State Board for Licensing Contractors
- For home warranties: Compare sample contracts side-by-side before purchasing; check caps, exclusions, waiting periods, service fees [^6^]

---

### Fixer 3: Payment Protection and Escrow

**Description:** Structuring payments to minimize risk of nonperformance.

**Actions:**
- Never pay more than 10-20% deposit (or $1,000, whichever is less) before work begins
- Tie remaining payments to completion milestones
- Withhold final payment (10-15%) until work is complete and passes inspection
- For large projects: Consider escrow arrangements
- Use credit cards (not cash/check) for deposit — provides dispute/chargeback rights
- Document all payments with receipts

---

### Fixer 4: Evidence Preservation Package

**Description:** Systematic documentation of all project-related evidence.

**Actions:**
- Photograph all work areas before construction begins ("before" photos)
- Document work in progress with dated photos/videos
- Keep copies of all contracts, change orders, permits, inspection reports
- Save all correspondence (email, text, voicemail) with contractor
- Record license plate numbers and business card information
- Note names of all workers who appear on-site
- For warranty claims: Document all maintenance records, service calls, claim denials in writing

---

### Fixer 5: Regulatory Complaint Pathways

**Description:** Understanding and utilizing appropriate complaint channels.

**Channel Hierarchy:**
1. **State Contractor Licensing Board** — Primary for licensed contractor issues; can issue citations, suspend/revoke licenses
   - California CSLB: Online complaint filing; 10,399 complaints referred for investigation FY2024-25 [^4^]
   - Tennessee: 829 complaints received FY2025 [^10^]
   - Nevada NSCB: Investigations unit for both licensed and unlicensed complaints [^15^]
2. **State Attorney General Consumer Protection Division** — For deceptive practices, fraud
   - Illinois AG saved consumers $12M via mediation + $48M via enforcement in 2024 [^2^]
   - North Carolina DOJ secured $84M+ in consumer protection settlements [^1^]
3. **Local/County Consumer Protection Office** — For mediation and resolution
   - Hillsborough County, Palm Beach County, Pinellas County (FL), Fairfax County (VA), Howard County (MD) all demonstrated successful mediation outcomes [^1^]
4. **FTC** — For interstate fraud, deceptive financing, home improvement scams
   - Report at ReportFraud.ftc.gov; 81,925 home improvement scam reports in 2024 [^5^]
5. **CFPB** — For home improvement financing, mortgage, and loan-related issues
   - ConsumerFinance.gov/complaint; ~3.19M complaints processed in 2024 [^3^]
6. **BBB** — For pattern documentation and business reputation tracking
   - BBB tracks complaint patterns and issues alerts (e.g., Like New Home Warranty pattern alert) [^22^]
7. **District Attorney / Criminal Prosecution** — For unlicensed contracting, fraud, forgery
   - CSLB referred 1,066 cases to prosecutors in FY2024-25 [^4^]
   - Florida unlicensed contracting is **felony during state of emergency** [^1^]

---

### Fixer 6: Post-Disaster Specific Protections

**Description:** Heightened vigilance after natural disasters when scam activity spikes.

**Actions:**
- Be aware that unlicensed contracting is enhanced to **felony** during declared emergencies in Florida [^1^]
- Do not accept unsolicited offers for work or inspections
- Contact your insurance company before hiring repair contractors
- Get multiple estimates for repair work
- Check that contractors have proper licenses for post-disaster work
- Report suspected unlicensed activity to local consumer protection and law enforcement
- Pinellas County model: Multi-agency sting operations with local law enforcement [^1^]

---

### Fixer 7: Lien Dispute Resolution

**Description:** Protecting against improper mechanics liens.

**Actions:**
- Require lien waivers from contractor and all subcontractors with each payment
- Verify no outstanding liens before making final payment
- If improper lien filed: Consult attorney; most states have strict notice and timing requirements for valid liens
- NCS Credit recommends: "Serve notices, secure mechanic's liens and bond claims, and file UCCs on all open accounts" [^7^]
- Record all payments and maintain proof of payment

---

### Fixer 8: Insurance Claim Documentation

**Description:** Maximizing success of property insurance claims for repairs.

**Actions:**
- Homeowner policy lawsuits (excluding hurricanes) exceeded **3,500 in federal courts in 2024** — highest since 2009 [^13^]
- Document all damage with photos/video before any cleanup
- Keep receipts for all emergency repairs
- Obtain independent estimates (not just insurance adjuster's)
- If claim denied: Request written denial with specific policy citations; consider state DOI complaint
- Lex Machina data shows homeowner coverage disputes climbing every year since 2018 [^13^]

## Part 7: Source Limitations and Gaps

### Acknowledged Limitations

1. **No single federal database for contractor complaints.** Unlike credit reporting or banking, contractor/workmanship complaints are fragmented across 50+ state licensing boards, county offices, and municipal inspection departments.

2. **CFPB data does not specifically break out "home improvement" as a standalone product category.** Home improvement loans are distributed across mortgage, personal loan, and "other" categories, making precise volume extraction difficult. The CFPB received over 3 million complaints in 2024 but the vast majority (85%) were credit/consumer reporting [^3^].

3. **State licensing board data varies dramatically in accessibility.** Some states (California, Tennessee) publish detailed enforcement reports. Others provide minimal or irregular data. No standardized national reporting framework exists.

4. **Municipal permitting/inspection data is highly localized.** Building inspection failure rates, permit violation data, and code enforcement actions are maintained at the city/county level and rarely aggregated state-wide or nationally.

5. **Home warranty complaint data is fragmented.** No single federal regulator has clear jurisdiction; complaints flow to state DOIs, FTC, CFPB, and BBB with no unified tracking.

6. **Construction defect litigation data relies on private analytics.** Lex Machina, AAA, and JAMS data are proprietary. Court filing data is public but difficult to aggregate nationally for this specific category.

7. **The CFA survey covers only 41 of 200+ state/local consumer agencies.** Actual nationwide complaint volume is significantly higher than the 350,000 reported [^1^].

8. **No comprehensive HUD/FHA property standards enforcement data was found** for 2024-2025 in accessible form. HUD FHEO data focuses on fair housing discrimination rather than property condition standards.

9. **Data lag.** Most comprehensive 2024 data was published in mid-to-late 2025. True "real-time" data does not exist in this domain.

### Areas Requiring Additional Research

- State-by-state contractor licensing board complaint databases and APIs
- Municipal building department inspection failure rates (pilot: 3-5 major cities)
- Home warranty company-specific complaint ratios by state DOI
- Mechanic's lien filing data at county recorder offices
- Construction defect arbitration data (AAA, JAMS — may require subscription)
- Insurance claim denial rates by major homeowners carriers
